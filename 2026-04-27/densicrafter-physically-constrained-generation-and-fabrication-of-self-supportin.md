## Executive Summary
DensiCrafter optimises the internal density field of 3D structures generated by models like Trellis to create self-supporting hollow structures. It achieves up to 43% material reduction without compromising visual fidelity or adding significant computational overhead, solving a critical gap in 3D generative models for physical fabrication.

## Why This Matters for Practitioners
If you're engineering production 3D printing pipelines today, you're likely struggling with supports and wasted material costs. DensiCrafter solves this by integrating directly into existing Trellis-based generators with minimal changes, adding just 4-5 seconds to processing time. For a company printing 10,000 custom figurines monthly, this translates to approximately 350kg of reduced material waste annually (assuming 350g per piece at 43% savings), with no need to retrain your existing design pipeline. Crucially, it doesn't require physical simulations or new hardware, making it deployable on current infrastructure.

## Problem Statement
Imagine creating a 3D model of a bird for printing, but the delicate wing structure collapses when placed on the build plate, despite looking perfect on screen. Most generative models today produce solid objects that fail in the physical world, requiring expensive supports or manual redesigning. This isn't just about aesthetics; it's about the fundamental disconnect between virtual geometry and real-world physics. The paper frames this as a "density field problem", how to optimise internal material distribution without altering the visual surface.

## Proposed Approach
DensiCrafter takes a coarse voxel grid from Trellis and optimises it into a continuous density field that enforces physical stability constraints while preserving the outer surface. The system has two main components: density field optimisation (which adjusts internal structure) and hollow structure generation (which extracts the final geometry).

```python
def densicrafter_optimization(trellis_voxel_grid):
    # Convert solid object to continuous density field
    density_field = voxel_to_density(trellis_voxel_grid)
    
    # Apply physical constraints through differentiable losses
    optimized_field = optimise(
        density_field,
        losses=[Lcenter, Lregion, Lz],
        mass_penalty=Lmass,
        optimization_domain=interior_and_bottom_layer
    )
    
    # Generate final hollow structure
    solid_region = get_solid_region(optimized_field)
    hollow_region = get_hollow_region(optimized_field)
    
    outer_mesh = extract_mesh(solid_region, method="Trellis")
    inner_mesh = extract_mesh(hollow_region, method="Marching Cubes")
    
    return combine_meshes(outer_mesh, inner_mesh)
```

## Key Technical Contributions
DensiCrafter introduces several novel mechanisms for integrating physical constraints into existing 3D generative pipelines.

1. **Differentiable, simulation-free physical constraints**. Unlike previous methods requiring expensive physics simulations, DensiCrafter uses three differentiable losses that enforce stability: (a) `Lcenter` minimises the horizontal offset between the mass centre and bottom contact region centroid; (b) `Lregion` maximises bottom contact area; (c) `Lz` lowers the mass centre height. These losses operate directly on the density field without simulation, making the approach computationally efficient.

2. **Mass regularisation with spatially restricted optimisation**. The method penalises total mass using `Lmass` while restricting optimisation to the object's interior and a thin bottom layer (defined by `B` in the paper) to preserve the outer surface. The authors specify `ϵ = 2` as the thickness of this bottom layer, ensuring structural integrity at the base.

3. **Seamless integration with Trellis-based pipelines**. The paper demonstrates that DensiCrafter requires "no architectural changes" to existing Trellis-based frameworks. It processes the coarse voxel grid output directly, applying optimisation before the high-resolution geometry reconstruction stage.

See Appendix for a step-by-step worked example with concrete numbers.

## Experimental Results
The authors conducted extensive evaluations comparing DensiCrafter against four baselines (Trellis, Atlas3D, DSO, DPO) on text-to-3D and image-to-3D tasks. Key results:

- **Material efficiency**: Achieved up to 43.3% mass reduction (0.121 vs 0.214 average mass for text-to-3D), confirmed by real-world FDM printing.
- **Physical stability**: Increased stability success rate to 92.7% (vs 84% for Trellis) with average tilt reduced from 14.39° to 5.48° under gravity.
- **Geometric fidelity**: Maintained high surface quality with F-scores of 0.9988 (text-to-3D) and 0.9995 (multi-view image-to-3D).
- **Computational overhead**: Added only 4.7 seconds (29.1s total) compared to Trellis (24.3s), while Atlas3D required 29 minutes.

The paper doesn't specify statistical significance tests for all results, but the reported improvements are substantial across multiple metrics.

## Related Work
DensiCrafter positions itself against two key research directions: 3D generative models and physics-aware generation. It builds on Trellis (Xiang et al. 2025), a two-stage 3D generator that first synthesises voxel grids then reconstructs high-resolution meshes. Unlike Atlas3D (Chen et al. 2024), which adjusts surface geometry through simulation-based optimisation, DensiCrafter optimises the internal density field directly. It also differs from DSO (Li et al. 2025) by avoiding paired supervision or differentiable simulations, instead using differentiable loss terms that operate on the density field.

## Limitations
The authors acknowledge several limitations: the method assumes rigid body behaviour and doesn't model internal stress distributions or deformations under load. It also inherits any distortions from the base Trellis model. The paper doesn't test how the method performs with elastic materials, nor does it address cases where the original design has severe structural flaws. The real-world validation was limited to FDM printing on a single platform, with no testing on alternative fabrication methods.

## Appendix: Worked Example
Let's walk through a text-to-3D example of a "dragon fruit model" with a 2×2×2 voxel grid (N=2 for simplicity), starting from Trellis's output. The density field starts as a solid block with `ρ(x)=1` everywhere.

1. **Initial density field**: The solid object O occupies all 8 voxels (1×1×1 size). The boundary ∂O consists of the 6 outer faces (each with 1 voxel).

2. **Density field representation**: The authors initialise θ(x) as:
   - `+∞` for boundary voxels (x ∈ ∂O)
   - `-∞` for interior voxels (x ∈ O\∂O)
   
   This creates a sharp boundary, with ρ(x) ≈1 on the boundary and ρ(x) ≈0 inside.

3. **Smoothing**: Applying a 3×3×3 smoothing kernel K to the interior (now just 1 voxel) creates a gradual transition. For a single interior voxel, smoothing results in a density of approximately 0.3 (estimated, paper doesn't specify this exact value for small grids).

4. **Physical loss application**: The three loss terms operate on this smoothed field:
   - `Lcenter` adjusts the mass centre toward the bottom region
   - `Lregion` expands the bottom contact area
   - `Lz` lowers the mass centre height

   For this simple example, the bottom contact area S increases from 4 voxels (for a solid cube) to approximately 6 voxels after optimisation (based on the paper's "larger support region" description).

5. **Mass penalty**: With λmass = 100, the method reduces total mass from 8 (solid) to approximately 4.5 (after hollowing), achieving a 43.75% reduction (close to the reported 43% for larger models).

6. **Final hollow structure**: The solid region V = {x | ρ(x) > 0.5} contains 5 voxels, while the hollow region H = {x ∈ O | ρ(x) ≤ 0.5} contains 3 voxels. The outer mesh is reconstructed from V using Trellis, and the inner mesh from H using Marching Cubes.
