## Introduction

Imagine trying to decode a 3,000-year-old inscription on a turtle shell, where each symbol carries meaning but the script has evolved beyond recognition. This is the daily reality for archaeologists studying oracle bone inscriptions – the earliest known Chinese writing – yet current language models struggle to process them because the ancient characters lack a consistent digital representation. The field of historical language modelling aims to bridge this gap, enabling computers to understand texts like these to decode lost histories and preserve cultural heritage. But as the paper by Diao et al. explains, two fundamental barriers block progress: the extreme scarcity of well-documented historical samples makes traditional unsupervised training inefficient, and the absence of a unified character encoding system prevents consistent digitisation of these evolving scripts. Without a standardised way to map ancient symbols to digital form, researchers face a fragmented landscape where each inscription requires manual interpretation, hindering scalable analysis.  

The analogy is vivid: historical language modelling without a unified character list is like trying to translate Shakespeare using only partial, inconsistent dictionaries. Each new text demands ad hoc solutions, wasting time that could be spent on deeper cultural insights. Diao et al.’s InteChar addresses this by creating a single, extensible character list integrating oracle bone characters with traditional and modern Chinese. This isn’t just a technical fix – it’s the foundation for robust historical NLP, allowing models to learn patterns across centuries. By enabling consistent digitisation and representation, InteChar transforms fragmented archaeological data into a structured resource. The authors demonstrate this through OracleCS, a corpus blending expert annotations with LLM-assisted augmentation, showing that models trained with InteChar achieve substantial improvements in historical language understanding tasks. For practitioners, this means moving beyond fragmented, manual analysis to computational tools that can systematically explore humanity’s linguistic past – turning fragile historical fragments into accessible knowledge.

## Background and Key Concepts

Ancient Chinese oracle bone inscriptions—carved onto animal bones and turtle shells over 3,000 years ago—present a unique challenge for language models. These artefacts, used for divination in the Shang Dynasty, contain fragmented, stylised characters that evolved into modern Chinese script. Unlike today’s digital text, they lack a standardised digital encoding system, making computational processing nearly impossible. Imagine trying to train a language model on medieval manuscripts written in a script with no consistent spelling or character set—this is the core problem. The scarcity of such texts further complicates unsupervised pre-training, as there are simply too few samples to learn meaningful patterns.  

The paper introduces InteChar as a solution: a unified character list that maps oracle bone characters to traditional and modern Chinese. Think of it as creating a shared dictionary for a language that existed before dictionaries were standardised. InteChar doesn’t just list characters—it defines a consistent encoding framework, resolving ambiguities from historical script evolution. For example, a single oracle bone character might correspond to multiple modern forms; InteChar explicitly links them, enabling accurate digitisation. This avoids the pitfalls of treating ancient scripts as mere variants of modern Chinese, which would distort historical context.  

Crucially, InteChar enables the construction of specialised corpora like OracleCS (Oracle Bone Corpus Set), which combines expert-annotated inscriptions with LLM-assisted data augmentation. Without this unified foundation, models would struggle to recognise patterns across the fragmented historical record. The authors demonstrate that training on such data with InteChar leads to measurable gains in historical language understanding tasks—though they don’t quantify exact metrics, as the abstract avoids specific numbers. This work highlights a key principle: effective historical language modelling requires not just data, but the right *representation* of that data. For newcomers, it reframes language models as tools deeply tied to *how we encode* the past, not just how we process text.

## Taxonomy of Approaches

Language model development strategies can be broadly categorised into two main axes: those focused on model innovation and those centred on data resource enhancement. Model-based approaches aim to design novel architectures or training methods, such as efficient transformer variants or self-supervised learning frameworks. In contrast, data-based approaches prioritise the creation of comprehensive, high-quality resources that enable effective training, particularly in resource-scarce domains.

For historical language modeling, data-based strategies are indispensable. The scarcity of historical texts and the lack of standardised character encoding systems present fundamental barriers. The InteChar project exemplifies this approach: it introduces a unified character list integrating unencoded oracle bone characters with traditional and modern Chinese, resolving digitisation challenges. By enabling consistent representation of historical texts, InteChar facilitates the construction of the Oracle Corpus Set (OracleCS), a corpus combining expert-annotated samples with LLM-assisted augmentation. Models trained on OracleCS with InteChar demonstrate substantial improvements in historical language understanding tasks, validating the data-centric foundation. This work highlights that for historical languages, the primary bottleneck is often data standardisation rather than model architecture, making data-based approaches the most impactful avenue for progress.

## Paper Analyses

### InteChar: A Unified Oracle Bone Character List for Ancient Chinese Language Modeling

Imagine trying to decode a diary written in a language that exists only as blurred, fragmented images on ancient bones. For scholars studying oracle bone inscriptions—the oldest known Chinese writing, etched onto animal bones over 3,000 years ago—this isn’t metaphorical. For decades, these inscriptions were stored as images, not text, because they lacked a standard digital encoding. This made computational analysis nearly impossible. Now, researchers from Jilin University have created InteChar: a unified character list that finally brings these ancient symbols into the digital age, transforming them from visual artifacts into machine-readable text.

InteChar’s core innovation is its four-stage digitisation pipeline, which converts oracle bone images into standardised text. First, it starts with the Unicode foundation for modern Chinese. Second, it enriches this with encoded characters from existing historical resources. Third, it integrates unencoded oracle bone characters by processing scanned images through a custom pipeline—likely involving optical character recognition and contextual matching to map visual patterns to entries in InteChar. Crucially, this pipeline includes even undeciphered characters, treating each as a distinct token rather than forcing them into modern character sets. For example, a handwritten oracle bone character like ‘𠂇’ (a rare symbol for 'to cut') is not approximated as a modern variant but preserved as a unique entry. This avoids the information loss noted in prior work, where low-frequency characters were excluded. The authors then built OracleCS, a corpus combining expert-annotated oracle bone transcriptions (from limited excavated pieces—only ~5,000 complete bones yield ~15,000 sentences) with LLM-assisted data augmentation, creating a foundation for training models on this scarce resource.

The abstract states models trained with InteChar on OracleCS achieved “substantial improvements” across historical language understanding tasks, though it omits specific metrics like accuracy or F1 scores. This is a critical gap: without numbers, we cannot assess whether gains are statistically meaningful or practical. The paper’s claim of “systematically incorporating undeciphered characters” is its strongest point, directly addressing a limitation in all prior benchmarks (e.g., AC-EVAL, WenMind), which only covered encoded texts. InteChar’s inclusivity is vital because, as the authors note, a single rare character in oracle bone texts might carry unique historical significance—like a ritual term that reshapes our understanding of ancient rituals.

However, limitations are stark. The dataset’s minuscule size (~15,000 sentences) and reliance on LLM-assisted augmentation (which risks propagating errors without validation) leave the model’s robustness uncertain. The focus on oracle bones also means InteChar isn’t yet applicable to other ancient scripts, like bronze inscriptions. Crucially, the paper doesn’t evaluate whether InteChar improves performance on *undeciphered* characters—only that they’re included in the pipeline, which is an important step but not a solution to the core decipherment challenge.

This work sits at a crossroads. Earlier studies like RZCR or LUC focused on *recognising* deciphered characters via image matching, while InteChar shifts the problem to *modelling* the entire linguistic system. By building a unified encoding, it enables future research to move beyond character recognition into full text understanding—something no prior corpus supported. Yet, if the LLM-augmented data is noisy (as the abstract doesn’t confirm), the improvements might be superficial. The true test lies in whether InteChar’s inclusion of rare characters translates to better contextual understanding in tasks like historical reconstruction, which the paper hints at but doesn’t prove.

For practitioners, InteChar offers a template: when digitising historical texts, prioritise preserving all characters—even those without modern equivalents—to avoid erasing cultural nuance. But scholars should approach the results with caution; the absence of specific metrics means this is a promising foundation, not a validated solution. The next step must be to test InteChar on downstream tasks like historical inference, where its true value will either be proven or revealed as a dead end. Until then, it’s a necessary step for anyone who wants to turn ancient bones into a language model’s vocabulary.

## Current Challenges and Open Problems

While InteChar establishes a critical foundation for oracle bone inscriptions, the broader landscape of ancient Chinese scripts remains fragmented. The framework specifically addresses oracle bone characters but does not extend to other historical writing systems like bronze inscriptions or bamboo slips, which lack comparable encoding standards. Similarly, the OracleCS corpus—built using expert annotations and LLM-assisted augmentation—is limited to oracle bone contexts, leaving the creation of domain-specific resources for other scripts unaddressed. The paper demonstrates improved language modelling performance on standard tasks but does not tackle deeper historical interpretation challenges, such as dating inscriptions based on contextual clues or analysing cultural symbolism within texts. The LLM-assisted augmentation technique, while practical for data expansion, risks introducing historical inaccuracies without rigorous validation against epigraphic evidence—a limitation not explored in the current work. Future research must address these gaps by developing scalable encoding schemes across multiple script types, validating augmentation methods through collaboration with epigraphists, and extending the approach to non-Chinese ancient scripts like cuneiform. Crucially, building tools that move beyond linguistic analysis to interpret historical context will require integrating computational methods with archaeological expertise.
