## Introduction

Imagine navigating a bustling market where vendors suddenly veer into your path, pedestrians dart between stalls, and cyclists swerve unpredictably. In autonomous driving, the ‘predictor’ that forecasts these movements isn’t just guessing paths—it’s the safety net for the entire vehicle. Most current systems rely on metrics like *Average Displacement Error (ADE)* or *Final Displacement Error (FDE)*, which measure how closely predicted paths match reality after the fact. But as Da et al. show, this is like judging a chess player solely on how many pieces they moved, ignoring whether their moves actually prevent checkmate. The real danger isn’t *how far off* a prediction is—it’s whether it captures *all possible directions* a pedestrian might take, so the self-driving vehicle (SDV) can choose a safe path *before* a collision. For instance, a pedestrian might step left, right, or even backtrack; a good predictor must reflect this diversity, not just average a single trajectory. The authors’ key insight is that evaluation must mirror real-world driving: in a high-risk scenario like a school zone, diversity (covering multiple possible actions) matters more than pinpoint accuracy, while in a quiet highway, accuracy dominates. Their pipeline dynamically weights these two dimensions based on scenario criticality, creating a score that *actually correlates with how safely the SDV drives*. This isn’t about fancier algorithms—it’s about asking the right question: *What does a prediction need to do to keep people safe, not just look good on a leaderboard?* Current metrics fail this test, leaving developers to choose predictors that might seem accurate but could miss the critical turn a pedestrian takes. The field’s next step isn’t better prediction—it’s better *evaluation* that puts safety, not statistics, at the wheel.

## Background and Key Concepts

In autonomous driving, planning transforms observed surroundings into actionable movement paths for the vehicle. At its core, this process relies on **trajectory prediction**—a module that forecasts the future paths of nearby agents like cars, pedestrians, or cyclists using their historical movement data. Imagine a tennis player anticipating an opponent’s shot: the planner needs to predict where other road users *will be* to position the ego vehicle safely, not just where they *were*.  

The standard autonomous driving pipeline operates in four sequential stages. First, the **perception module** detects agents and their current positions. Second, the **trajectory predictor** generates possible future paths (trajectories) for each agent—typically as a set of equally likely options rather than a single line. Third, the **planning module** uses these predictions to calculate the ego vehicle’s optimal path, factoring in traffic rules and map geometry. Finally, the **control module** executes the plan. Crucially, the quality of the trajectory prediction directly dictates the planner’s ability to avoid collisions or maintain smooth flow: a flawed prediction could lead to a sudden emergency brake or an unsafe lane change.  

Current evaluation of trajectory predictors remains flawed. Researchers traditionally measure success via **error-based metrics** like Average Displacement Error (ADE) or Final Displacement Error (FDE), which quantify the average distance between predicted and actual paths *after the fact*. But as the paper demonstrates, these metrics ignore whether the prediction actually supports safer decisions in real driving. A predictor might score well on ADE by consistently predicting a single path (e.g., a car continuing straight), yet miss critical alternatives (e.g., a pedestrian darting across the road)—a gap that risks safety but goes unnoticed. The paper argues that evaluation must instead reflect how predictions *impact* the vehicle’s driving performance, especially in high-risk scenarios like intersections. This shift from post-hoc accuracy to *actionable* foresight is the core challenge for modern planning systems.

## Taxonomy of Approaches

The taxonomy of trajectory prediction evaluation approaches divides methodologies along two axes: *metric type* and *scenario awareness*. Traditional methods fall under **error-based metrics**, relying solely on post-hoc distance measures like ADE (Average Displacement Error) and FDE (Final Displacement Error), which quantify prediction deviation without linking to downstream decision quality (e.g., Chen et al., 2020). These fail to capture how prediction quality influences autonomous vehicle safety in complex interactions.  

A newer class, **scenario-driven evaluation**, addresses this gap by dynamically assessing predictors through dimensions relevant to real-world driving contexts. The paper *Measuring What Matters* (Da et al., AAAI 2026) exemplifies this category. It proposes a pipeline evaluating both *accuracy* (how closely forecasts match ground truth) and *diversity* (whether predictions cover plausible agent movement directions), then integrates these into a single score weighted by scenario criticality—such as high-risk intersections versus low-speed highways. Crucially, this evaluation is validated via a *closed-loop benchmark* using real-world driving data, demonstrating that their method better correlates with actual SDV (self-driving vehicle) performance than conventional metrics. This approach shifts evaluation from abstract error reduction to measurable impact on vehicle safety, making it the first framework to operationalise *cautious decision-making* as an evaluation criterion.

## Paper Analyses

### Measuring What Matters: Scenario-Driven Evaluation for Trajectory Predictors in Autonomous Driving

Trajectory prediction in autonomous driving has long relied on error metrics like ADE and FDE, which measure geometric distance between predictions and ground truth. Da et al. challenge this norm by demonstrating that such metrics fail to capture how prediction quality actually affects driving performance in real-world scenarios. Their core insight is that the value of a prediction depends entirely on the context: in simple highway driving, accuracy matters most, but in complex intersections where multiple agent behaviours are possible, diversity—covering all plausible paths—is crucial for safe planning. This contextual dependency is what current benchmarks miss.

The authors propose ED-Eva, a scenario-driven evaluation framework that dynamically weights accuracy and diversity based on traffic criticality. Its mechanism hinges on two innovations. First, they design an adaptive classifier (using vehicles’ interaction graphs as input) that outputs a weighting factor determining whether to prioritise prediction accuracy (in low-risk scenarios) or diversity (in high-risk interactions). Second, they introduce GMM-Area Diversity (GAD) to quantify prediction spread robustly. GAD fits a Gaussian Mixture Model to predicted trajectories and computes the area of the resulting uncertainty ellipse—measuring spatial coverage without relying on ground truth. This avoids the pitfalls of existing diversity metrics (like AMV) that assume known ground truth, which isn’t available in closed-loop systems.

Critically, ED-Eva operates in a closed-loop setting, evaluating how prediction quality influences actual planner decisions. The paper’s key evidence comes from correlating prediction metrics with driving performance (e.g., lane changes, braking smoothness), showing ADE’s weak relationship with outcomes (Figure 3). While the abstract confirms ED-Eva achieves “higher correlation with real driving outcomes” than error-based metrics, it does not specify exact correlation coefficients or report quantitative gains on particular predictors. The evaluation used a real-world dataset (referenced as Caesar et al. 2020), but the abstract omits dataset size or specific scenarios tested—only noting 9059 scenarios were preliminarily studied.

The work’s strength lies in its contextual awareness: it moves beyond isolated prediction accuracy to align evaluation with the system’s ultimate goal—safe driving. This contrasts with prior diversity metrics (e.g., AMV, energy scores), which assess spread in open-loop settings without considering how diversity impacts planning outcomes. ED-Eva’s adaptive weighting also addresses a gap in scenario-criticality research, which previously relied on predefined scenario categories rather than dynamic assessment.

Limitations are clear from the abstract. The framework requires a pre-trained scenario classifier (not detailed), and while GAD is novel, its implementation details (e.g., GMM component count) aren’t specified. Crucially, the paper doesn’t quantify *how much* better ED-Eva correlates with driving performance—only that it’s superior to ADE/FDE. Without this, its practical impact remains theoretical. The closed-loop experiments also lack comparisons to other scenario-adaptive methods, leaving open whether the adaptive classifier is necessary or if simpler heuristics would suffice.

For context, ED-Eva extends but doesn’t replace existing trajectory predictors like MTR or LaneRCNN—it’s an evaluation tool, not a prediction model. It builds on works like Grewal et al. (2024) that stress diversity in critical scenarios, but adds the first rigorous method for dynamically balancing accuracy and diversity. In practice, this means developers could now select predictors not just for low ADE, but for those that generate diverse outputs *when it matters most*—like at intersections—without over-prioritising it on empty highways.

The paper’s greatest contribution is reframing evaluation: prediction quality isn’t absolute, but a function of context. This aligns with the reality that autonomous systems must balance efficiency (highway accuracy) and safety (junction diversity), yet the abstract’s lack of specific correlation numbers or dataset details limits its immediate usability. Future work should quantify ED-Eva’s gains and test it across diverse real-world scenarios—something the authors acknowledge as needed. For now, it offers a principled path to evaluation that finally measures what matters: the impact on actual driving.

## Current Challenges and Open Problems

Current evaluation practices for trajectory predictors in autonomous driving remain fundamentally misaligned with real-world safety needs, as they rely solely on error-based metrics (e.g., ADE, FDE) that measure post-hoc accuracy without accounting for how predictions influence the self-driving vehicle’s (SDV) decision-making in complex interactions. While the scenario-driven framework proposed by Da et al. advances evaluation by weighting accuracy and diversity through scenario criticality, several challenges persist. The framework currently supports predictor selection but does not address how to integrate scenario-specific evaluation into the training loop—leaving a gap between performance metrics and model development. Furthermore, criticality assessment depends on dataset-specific closed-loop benchmarks, raising questions about generalisability to unobserved scenarios like extreme weather or rare pedestrian behaviours. The paper also doesn’t resolve how to dynamically compute criticality in real-time during deployment, where computational constraints may hinder the framework’s scalability. Crucially, the evaluation remains anchored to a single real-world dataset; its applicability across diverse geographic regions, traffic cultures, or sensor modalities (e.g., lidar vs. camera-only systems) remains untested. These unresolved issues underscore the need for evaluation methods that evolve with both the driving environment and the predictor’s role within the SDV pipeline, rather than merely measuring static output quality.
